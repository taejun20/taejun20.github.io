Today I read an essay, "As We May Think" (1945) and share what I felt with some direct quotes.

# Vannevar Bush (1890-1974)

![|50%](img/posts/260714-vannevar-bush-as-we-may-think/bush.jpg)

Vannevar Bush was an American engineer and scientist. He was a professor at MIT from 1919 and later dean of engineering. During World War II, he coordinated the work of about six thousand scientists on major wartime research projects. After the war, he became a leading advocate for continued public support for scientific research and its benefits to society.

# The Postwar Vision

> "Now, for many, this [*the war*] appears to be approaching an end. What are the scientists to do next? ... They [*the scientists*] have done their part on the devices that made it possible to turn back the enemy. ... They have felt within themselves the stir of achievement. They have been part of a great team. Now, as peace approaches, one asks where they will find objectives worthy of their best."

The essay was published in July 1945, during World War II. Germany had just surrendered in May, and the world was only weeks away from ending the war in the Pacific in August. People must've felt the devastating power of technology and left questioning what role science should play in the future. It was at this moment that Bush wrote about how science and technology could be directed to make people's lives better.

# Information Explosion and Human Memory

> "There is a growing mountain of research. But there is increased evidence that we are being bogged down today as specialization extends. The investigator is staggered by the findings and conclusions of thousands of other workers—conclusions which he cannot find time to grasp, much less to remember, as they appear. ... **If the aggregate time spent in writing scholarly works and in reading them could be evaluated, the ratio between these amounts of time might well be startling**" *[1]*

> "Our ineptitude in getting at the record is largely caused by the artificiality of systems of indexing. When data of any sort are placed in storage, they are filed alphabetically or numerically, and information is found (when it is) by tracking it down from subclass to subclass." *[6]*

Bush first pointed to the approaching explosion of information. More knowledge than anyone could really consume. He then asks, "how much time do we spend reading than writing?" The problem, he argued, was not the growth of knowledge itself, but that the way it was organized and accessed had barely evolved (at the time of 1945). Keep in mind that there was no digital storage in 1945. Information was still "filed alphabetically" in office cabinets and libraries.

> "**The human mind does not work that way.** It operates by association. With one item in its grasp, it snaps instantly to the next that is suggested by the association of thoughts, in accordance with some **intricate web of trails** carried by the cells of the brain. ... the speed of action, the intricacy of trails, the detail of mental pictures, is awe-inspiring beyond all else in nature" *[6]*

This part is fascinating. Bush argued that the human mind doesn't work that way, and said it operates through **association and connection, forming an "intricate web of trails"**. Now we have the World Wide Web, where information points are connected through hyperlinks. We navigate from one idea to another instantly and flexibly, the very natural way that Bush talked about. His vision became so fundamental that billions of people use it everyday without thinking about it. Many ideas in computer science (and HCI) trace back to this vision of using computers to augment human thinking.

# Memex: Augmenting Human Thinking

![An illustration of memex (image from Life Magazine, 1945)|60%](img/posts/260714-vannevar-bush-as-we-may-think/memex.jpg)

> "Consider a future device for individual use, which is a sort of mechanized private file and library. It needs a name, and to coin one at random, "memex" will do. A memex is a device in which an individual stores all his books, records, and communications, and which is mechanized so that it may be consulted with exceeding speed and flexibility. It is an enlarged intimate supplement to his memory" *[6]*

> "On the top are slanting translucent screens, on which material can be projected for convenient reading. There is a keyboard, and sets of buttons and levers. ... In one end is the stored material. The matter of bulk is well taken care of by improved microfilm. ... Most of the memex contents are purchased on microfilm ready for insertion. Books of all sorts, newspapers, are thus obtained and dropped into place. ... Any given book of his library can thus be called up and consulted with far greater facility than if it were taken from a shelf." *[6]*

Again this was 1945, before ENIAC (the first general-purpose electronic computer, 1946) and about three decades before the graphical user interface (GUI). Here, Bush envisioned the Memex, a hypothetical machine that would serve as an extension of human memory. Reading it today, it essentially looks like a modern personal computer. The translucent screens Bush imagined are now our monitors and tablets, the keyboard and buttons are our input devices, and the microfilm is now semiconductor memory and cloud storage. I think Bush's greatest insight was not the technological details but something more fundamental: human need to own, organize, and instantly access their own collection of knowledge.

> "The process of tying two items together is the important thing. ... when one of these items is in view, the other can be instantly recalled merely by tapping a button below the corresponding code space." *[7]*

> "The owner of the memex, let us say, is interested in the origin and properties of the bow and arrow. Specifically he is studying why the short Turkish bow was apparently superior to the English long bow in the skirmishes of the Crusades. He has dozens of possibly pertinent books and articles in his memex. First he runs through an encyclopedia, finds an interesting but sketchy article, leaves it projected. Next, in a history, he finds another pertinent item, and ties the two together. Thus he goes, building a trail of many items. Occasionally he inserts a comment of his own, either linking it into the main trail of joining it by a side trail to a particular item. When it becomes evident that the elastic properties of available materials had a great deal to do with the bow, he branches off on a side trail which takes him through textbooks on elasticity and tables of physical constants. He inserts a page of longhand analysis of his own. **Thus he builds a trail of his interest through the maze of materials available to him.**" *[7]*

Bush illustrated an example workflow for how a person would use the Memex. It is clear that many of his ideas touched today's hyperlink-based internet, personal knowledge management systems, and the interactive user interfaces. He reached this vision from a simple but profound question: how should computers help people think?

# Brain-Computer Interface in 1945?

> "All our steps in creating or absorbing material of the record proceed through one of the senses—the tactile when we touch keys, the oral when we speak or listen, the visual when we read. **Is it not possible that some day the path may be established more directly?**" *[8]*

> "By bone conduction we already introduce sounds into the nerve channels of the deaf in order that they may hear. It is not possible that we may learn to introduce them without the present cumbersomeness of first transforming electrical vibrations to mechanical ones, which the human mechanism promptly transforms back to the electrical form? With a couple of electrodes on the skull the encephalograph now produces pen-and-ink traces which bear some relation to the electrical phenomena going on in the brain itself. ... Must we always transform to mechanical movements in order to proceed from one electrical phenomenon to another?" *[8]*

Near the end of the essay, Bush suddenly mentioned the possibility of direct neural communication between human and computer, saying, if both electronic devices and the human nervous system are electrical systems, why do we even convert the signals into mechanical forms in the middle. Going beyond modern computers, Bush's thought was touching on direct human-machine integration, a topic that is still in the early stages of research in 2026. I suddenly wondered: do we have anyone among us who can foresee the world of 2100 as accurately as Bush did ours?

# Concluding

> "The applications of science have built man a well-supplied house, and are teaching him to live healthily therein. They have enabled him to throw masses of people against another with cruel weapons. They may yet allow him truly to encompass the great record and to grow in the wisdom of race experience. He may perish in conflict before he learns to wield that record for his true good." *[8]*

Bush ends the essay with a warning: humanity may destroy itself through war before it learns to use its accumulated knowledge for the "true good". Written in the shadow of World War II, the warning feels no less urgent today.

In parallel, AI revolution is reshaping how we learn and think today. AI tools like ChatGPT and Claude have made knowledge more accessible than ever, connecting to the Bush's vision of using technology to extend human capability. But at the same time, we are facing a deeper question: are we truly augmenting human thinking, or are we merely automating it? It is hard to deny that AI is already carrying out much of the cognitive tasks on our behalf. Bush worried that science was producing knowledge faster than humans could absorb it. Today, search engines and AI assistants have made accessing information almost effortless, and I guess the challenge has shifted. It is no longer whether we can find knowledge quickly, but whether we are still doing the intellectual work of understanding it ourselves. The line between cognitive augmentation and cognitive replacement may seem thin, but I feel that will be the defining question of the AI era.
